The Idea: Learn Systems Through Stories

Every genetic disease is a natural experiment that reveals how the body works

Medical school admissions committees do not want students who memorized a textbook. They want students who can think across systems. The MCAT is designed to test exactly this: give a student a clinical scenario and see if they can trace the molecular, cellular, and physiological chain of events.

The most memorable way to learn these connections is through disease. When you understand why a sickle cell patient cannot concentrate their urine, you have simultaneously learned hemoglobin biochemistry, Mendelian genetics, renal countercurrent mechanisms, and population genetics. The disease is not an add-on to be memorized after learning the "real" biology. The disease is the biology, made visible.

Each disease in this guide was chosen because it teaches the maximum number of foundational MCAT concepts in a way that is inherently memorable. A single point mutation causing a glutamic acid to valine substitution is far more memorable than an abstract lecture on protein folding, and it teaches you more.

How to Use This Guide

Each disease page shows you the concepts it teaches as colored tags at the top. As you work through the diseases, you will notice the same foundational concepts appearing again and again in different contexts. That repetition across different disease stories is what builds deep understanding. By the time you have worked through all of them, you will have covered the major MCAT biology and biochemistry concepts multiple times, each from a different angle.

Sickle Cell Disease

A single amino acid substitution that teaches you half of biology
Autosomal Recessive
Protein StructureHemoglobin/O2 TransportMendelian GeneticsPopulation GeneticsRenal PhysiologyImmune Function

Sickle cell disease begins with the smallest possible change to a protein: a single nucleotide mutation in the beta-globin gene (GAG to GTG) that replaces glutamic acid (hydrophilic, negatively charged at pH 7.4) with valine (hydrophobic, nonpolar) at position 6. This one substitution transforms one of the most elegant molecules in the body into one of the most destructive.

MCAT Concept: Protein Structure and Function

This single mutation teaches primary, tertiary, and quaternary structure simultaneously. The primary structure change (Glu to Val) alters the surface chemistry of the protein. Under low-oxygen conditions, the hydrophobic valine on one HbS molecule inserts into a hydrophobic pocket on another HbS molecule, causing polymerization into rigid fibers. This is quaternary structure gone wrong: the same intermolecular forces that normally allow hemoglobin subunits to cooperate (hydrophobic interactions, van der Waals forces) now drive pathological aggregation. The rigid polymers deform the red blood cell from its normal biconcave disc into the characteristic sickle shape.

Normal hemoglobin (HbA) is a tetramer of two alpha and two beta subunits, each carrying a heme group with an iron atom that reversibly binds oxygen. Hemoglobin exhibits cooperative binding: when one subunit binds O2, it changes conformation (T state to R state), making it easier for the other subunits to bind O2. This produces the sigmoidal oxygen-hemoglobin dissociation curve. The Hill coefficient for hemoglobin is about 2.8 (positive cooperativity; n=1 would be hyperbolic like myoglobin).

In sickle cell disease, deoxygenated HbS polymerizes. This means sickling is triggered by conditions that promote the T (deoxy) state: low pO2, low pH (Bohr effect: H+ ions stabilize T state), high CO2, high temperature, and high 2,3-BPG. Every factor that shifts the curve right increases sickling risk.

The Domino Chain: One Mutation, Every System
GAG→GTG mutation→ Glu→Val at β-6→ HbS polymerizes when deoxy→ RBC sickling→ Vaso-occlusion

From vaso-occlusion, the damage radiates outward to every organ:

Splenic infarction→ Functional asplenia→ Susceptibility to encapsulated bacteria
Vasa recta sickling→ Medullary damage→ Cannot concentrate urine→ Dehydration→ More sickling
Chronic hemolysis→ Elevated bilirubin→ Jaundice + Pigment gallstones
Bone marrow expands→ "Crew cut" skull X-ray
MCAT Concept: Renal Physiology Through Sickle Cell

The renal medulla is the perfect storm for sickling: low oxygen tension, high osmolality (the countercurrent multiplier concentrates solutes to 1200 mOsm/kg at the tip of the loop of Henle), and acidic pH. Sickled RBCs in the vasa recta damage the countercurrent exchange system. Without a functioning countercurrent multiplier, the kidney cannot create the medullary concentration gradient needed to produce concentrated urine. Patients develop isosthenuria (urine fixed at ~300 mOsm, same as plasma). This means they cannot conserve water when dehydrated, leading to a vicious cycle: dehydration concentrates hemoglobin, promoting more sickling, causing more medullary damage.

If the MCAT gives you a passage about a patient who cannot concentrate urine and has a hemoglobin abnormality, this is the connection they are testing.

MCAT Concept: Immune Function and the Spleen

The spleen filters blood and is the primary site for clearing encapsulated bacteria (organisms with polysaccharide capsules that resist phagocytosis unless opsonized by antibodies). The spleen's marginal zone B cells produce IgM antibodies against polysaccharide antigens. In sickle cell disease, repeated splenic infarctions destroy the spleen by age 5 (functional asplenia). Without splenic filtration, patients are vulnerable to Streptococcus pneumoniae, Haemophilus influenzae, and Neisseria meningitidis. This is why sickle cell patients receive prophylactic penicillin and pneumococcal vaccines.

This teaches you splenic function, innate vs adaptive immunity, opsonization, and the role of IgM, all through one disease.

MCAT Concept: Population Genetics and Natural Selection

The sickle cell allele (HbS) persists at high frequency (up to 25% carrier rate) in sub-Saharan Africa and other malaria-endemic regions because of heterozygote advantage (also called balancing selection or overdominance). HbAS carriers have a survival advantage against Plasmodium falciparum malaria because the parasite cannot complete its life cycle as efficiently in cells containing HbS. HbAA individuals are fully susceptible to malaria; HbSS individuals have sickle cell disease. The heterozygote (HbAS) has the highest fitness in malaria-endemic environments.

This is the textbook example of Hardy-Weinberg equilibrium modification by natural selection. If q = frequency of HbS allele, then q^2 = frequency of sickle cell disease, 2pq = carrier frequency, p^2 = frequency of normal homozygotes. The MCAT will give you allele frequencies and ask you to calculate carrier rates, or vice versa.

MCAT Trap: Sickle Cell Trait vs. Disease

Sickle cell trait (HbAS, heterozygous) is generally asymptomatic under normal conditions. Carriers can experience sickling under extreme conditions (very high altitude, severe dehydration, extreme exercise). Sickle cell disease (HbSS, homozygous) produces the full clinical picture. The MCAT may describe a healthy athlete who develops symptoms only under extreme altitude and ask you to identify the genotype. The answer is HbAS (trait), not HbSS (disease).

What You Just Learned From One Disease

Protein primary, tertiary, and quaternary structure. Hydrophobic interactions driving pathology. Hemoglobin cooperativity (T and R states, sigmoidal curve, Hill coefficient). Oxygen-hemoglobin dissociation curve shifts (Bohr effect, 2,3-BPG, temperature, CO2). Renal countercurrent multiplier and concentrating ability. Splenic function and encapsulated bacteria. IgM, opsonization, innate vs adaptive immunity. Hardy-Weinberg equilibrium, heterozygote advantage, natural selection. Autosomal recessive inheritance, carrier frequency calculations. Bilirubin metabolism and gallstone formation. Bone marrow expansion (extramedullary hematopoiesis).

Cystic Fibrosis

The most common lethal autosomal recessive disease in Caucasians teaches you membrane biology
Autosomal Recessive
Membrane TransportProtein Folding/ProcessingAR InheritanceRespiratory PhysiologyPancreatic FunctionReproductive Biology

Cystic fibrosis is caused by mutations in the CFTR gene (cystic fibrosis transmembrane conductance regulator), which encodes a chloride channel on the apical membrane of epithelial cells. The most common mutation, delta-F508, is a deletion of phenylalanine at position 508. This does not destroy the protein's function directly. Instead, it causes the protein to misfold. The endoplasmic reticulum's quality control system (chaperones like calnexin and calreticulin) detects the misfolded protein, tags it with ubiquitin, and sends it to the proteasome for degradation. The channel never reaches the cell membrane.

MCAT Concept: Protein Processing and the ER Quality Control

This disease teaches the entire secretory pathway. Normally, CFTR is synthesized on ribosomes bound to the rough ER, threaded into the ER membrane co-translationally via the signal recognition particle (SRP), folded with the help of ER chaperones, glycosylated in the ER and Golgi, and trafficked to the plasma membrane via vesicles. The delta-F508 mutation disrupts folding in the ER, triggering ER-associated degradation (ERAD): misfolded proteins are retrotranslocated to the cytoplasm, polyubiquitinated, and degraded by the 26S proteasome. Understanding CF means understanding the ER, Golgi, vesicular transport, protein quality control, ubiquitin-proteasome pathway, and membrane protein insertion.

The Domino Chain: No Chloride Channel, Thick Mucus Everywhere

CFTR is a chloride channel (not a pump; it uses the electrochemical gradient, not ATP hydrolysis, to move Cl- ions, though ATP is used to gate the channel open). When CFTR is absent from the apical membrane, chloride cannot be secreted into the lumen. Water normally follows chloride osmotically to hydrate secretions. Without chloride (and therefore water) secretion, secretions become thick and viscous.

CFTR absent from membrane→ No Cl- secretion→ No water follows→ Thick, dehydrated secretions

This single mechanism produces disease in every organ with secretory epithelium:

Lungs→Thick mucus traps bacteria→Chronic infections (Pseudomonas, S. aureus)→Bronchiectasis, respiratory failure
Pancreas→Ducts clog with thick secretions→Enzymes cannot reach duodenum→Malabsorption (fat-soluble vitamins A, D, E, K)
Sweat glands→CFTR normally reabsorbs Cl- from sweat→Without CFTR, sweat is abnormally salty→Basis of the sweat chloride test
Vas deferens→Absent or atretic→Male infertility (98% of CF males)
MCAT Concept: Cholera Toxin and CFTR

Cholera toxin ADP-ribosylates the Gs alpha subunit, permanently activating adenylyl cyclase. cAMP rises, activating PKA, which phosphorylates and opens CFTR chloride channels. Massive Cl- secretion into the intestinal lumen drags water out, causing secretory diarrhea. This is the exact opposite of CF: cholera is too much CFTR activity, CF is no CFTR activity. The MCAT loves connecting these two. There is even a hypothesis that CF carriers (heterozygotes) have partial CFTR function that protects against cholera-induced fluid loss, explaining heterozygote advantage similar to sickle cell and malaria.

MCAT Concept: Fat-Soluble Vitamin Deficiency

Pancreatic insufficiency in CF means lipase cannot reach the small intestine. Without lipase, dietary fats are not digested into fatty acids and monoglycerides, so they cannot be absorbed. Fat-soluble vitamins (A, D, E, K) require fat absorption for their own uptake. Deficiency of each produces distinct pathology: Vitamin A (night blindness), Vitamin D (rickets/osteomalacia), Vitamin E (neurological problems, hemolytic anemia), Vitamin K (bleeding, because clotting factors II, VII, IX, X require vitamin K for gamma-carboxylation of glutamic acid residues). One disease, one mechanism (malabsorption), four separate vitamin deficiency syndromes.

What You Just Learned From One Disease

CFTR as a chloride channel (not a pump). Ion channel gating by ATP. Osmotic movement of water following solute. The entire secretory pathway (ER, Golgi, vesicles, membrane insertion). Protein misfolding and ER-associated degradation. Ubiquitin-proteasome pathway. GPCR signaling (cholera toxin, Gs, cAMP, PKA). Respiratory physiology (mucociliary clearance). Pancreatic exocrine function. Fat-soluble vitamin absorption and deficiency syndromes. Sweat gland physiology. Reproductive anatomy (vas deferens). Carrier frequency and heterozygote advantage. Autosomal recessive inheritance patterns.

Huntington Disease

The cruelty of autosomal dominant inheritance and the concept of anticipation
Autosomal Dominant
Trinucleotide RepeatsAD InheritanceNeurotransmissionAnticipationProtein AggregationBasal Ganglia

Huntington disease is caused by an expansion of the CAG trinucleotide repeat in the huntingtin gene on chromosome 4. CAG codes for glutamine, so the mutant protein contains a long polyglutamine (polyQ) tract. Normal individuals have 10-35 repeats. Disease occurs at 36+ repeats, with full penetrance above 40. The expanded polyQ tract causes the protein to misfold and aggregate into intranuclear inclusions that are toxic to neurons, particularly in the caudate nucleus of the basal ganglia.

MCAT Concept: Autosomal Dominant Inheritance

Only one mutant copy is needed for disease. Each child of an affected parent has a 50% chance of inheriting the allele. Unlike autosomal recessive diseases, there are no "carriers" who are phenotypically normal. However, Huntington has a cruel twist: symptoms typically appear around age 35-45, after most individuals have already had children. By the time a person knows they carry the gene, they may have already passed it on. This raises profound ethical questions about genetic testing, which the MCAT's psychology/sociology section may address.

Huntington demonstrates complete penetrance (100% of people with 40+ repeats develop the disease) but variable expressivity (age of onset and severity vary depending on repeat length).

MCAT Concept: Anticipation

Trinucleotide repeats are unstable during DNA replication. The repeat region can expand from one generation to the next, particularly during spermatogenesis (the many rounds of mitotic division in spermatogonia increase the chance of replication slippage). This means a father with 42 repeats may pass on 50+ repeats to his child. More repeats = more toxic protein = earlier onset = more severe disease. This phenomenon of increasing severity and earlier onset across generations is called anticipation.

If the MCAT shows a pedigree where each generation develops symptoms younger than the last, the answer is anticipation from trinucleotide repeat expansion.

MCAT Concept: Basal Ganglia and Motor Control

The hallmark of Huntington is chorea: involuntary, jerky, dance-like movements. This happens because the caudate and putamen (striatum) normally inhibit unwanted movements via the indirect pathway of the basal ganglia. In Huntington, GABAergic neurons in the striatum degenerate. GABA is the major inhibitory neurotransmitter of the CNS. When these inhibitory neurons die, the thalamus is disinhibited and sends excessive excitatory signals to the motor cortex, producing involuntary movements.

Compare this to Parkinson disease: loss of dopaminergic neurons in the substantia nigra pars compacta reduces excitation of the direct pathway, leading to difficulty initiating movement (bradykinesia, rigidity, tremor at rest). Huntington = too much movement (lost inhibition). Parkinson = too little movement (lost excitation). The MCAT loves comparing these two.

MCAT Trap: Trinucleotide Repeat Diseases Are Not All Alike
DiseaseRepeatInheritanceMechanism
HuntingtonCAG (polyQ)ADToxic gain of function (aggregation)
Fragile XCGGX-linked dominantGene silencing (methylation)
Myotonic dystrophyCTGADToxic RNA
Friedreich ataxiaGAAAR (the outlier)Gene silencing (heterochromatin)

Friedreich ataxia is the one that does not fit: it is autosomal recessive (all others are dominant or X-linked), and the GAA expansion silences the frataxin gene (loss of function) rather than creating a toxic protein (gain of function). The MCAT asks which trinucleotide repeat disorder is the exception.

What You Just Learned From One Disease

Autosomal dominant inheritance and penetrance vs expressivity. Trinucleotide repeat expansion mechanism (replication slippage). Anticipation. Protein misfolding and aggregation (compare to prion diseases, Alzheimer amyloid, alpha-synuclein in Parkinson). Basal ganglia circuitry (direct vs indirect pathways). GABAergic inhibitory neurotransmission. Comparison of Huntington (hyperkinetic) vs Parkinson (hypokinetic). Ethical considerations of presymptomatic genetic testing. Gain-of-function vs loss-of-function mutations.

Phenylketonuria (PKU)

An enzyme deficiency that teaches metabolism, cofactors, and the power of early intervention
Autosomal Recessive
Enzyme KineticsAmino Acid MetabolismAR InheritanceCofactors (BH4)NeurodevelopmentNewborn Screening

PKU is caused by deficiency of phenylalanine hydroxylase (PAH), the enzyme that converts phenylalanine to tyrosine. Without PAH, phenylalanine accumulates to toxic levels in the blood and brain. The enzyme requires tetrahydrobiopterin (BH4) as a cofactor and molecular oxygen as a co-substrate. This is a monooxygenase reaction: one atom of O2 is incorporated into tyrosine, and the other is reduced to water.

MCAT Concept: Enzyme Kinetics in Real Life

PKU patients have essentially zero PAH activity (Vmax approaches zero). No matter how much phenylalanine is present, it cannot be converted to tyrosine. This is the real-world equivalent of a Lineweaver-Burk plot where the enzyme is completely inhibited. Treatment is dietary restriction of phenylalanine (reducing substrate below toxic threshold) rather than adding more enzyme.

Some patients have a milder form caused by BH4 deficiency rather than PAH deficiency. These patients respond to BH4 supplementation. This teaches the MCAT concept of cofactor vs enzyme deficiency: if the enzyme is present but its required cofactor is missing, supplying the cofactor can restore activity. If the enzyme itself is absent/nonfunctional, the cofactor does not help.

The Domino Chain: Blocked Pathway, Toxic Accumulation
PAH deficient→Phe cannot become Tyr→Phe accumulates→Converted to phenylpyruvate (phenylketone)→Excreted in urine (musty smell)
Excess Phe in brain→Competes with other large neutral amino acids for transport across BBB→Reduced tryptophan uptake→Reduced serotonin synthesis
Tyrosine now essential→Less melanin production→Fair skin, light hair
Less tyrosine→Less dopamine, NE, epi synthesis→Neurotransmitter deficits
MCAT Concept: Essential vs Nonessential Amino Acids

Phenylalanine is an essential amino acid (humans cannot synthesize it; must be obtained from diet). Tyrosine is normally nonessential because it is made from phenylalanine. But in PKU, the conversion is blocked, making tyrosine conditionally essential. This is a favorite MCAT concept: the classification of an amino acid as essential or nonessential can change depending on the metabolic context. Similarly, arginine is nonessential in adults but essential in infants (they cannot synthesize enough via the urea cycle).

MCAT Concept: Blood-Brain Barrier Transport

Large neutral amino acids (Phe, Tyr, Trp, Leu, Ile, Val) share a common transporter (LAT1) across the blood-brain barrier. When phenylalanine is extremely elevated, it saturates the transporter, competitively excluding other amino acids from entering the brain. This reduces brain levels of tryptophan (precursor to serotonin) and tyrosine (precursor to dopamine, norepinephrine, epinephrine). This is competitive inhibition in a physiological context: excess Phe acts like a competitive inhibitor for the transport of other amino acids across the BBB.

MCAT Trap: Maternal PKU

A woman with PKU who is not on dietary restriction has high blood phenylalanine. If she becomes pregnant, her excess Phe crosses the placenta and damages the developing fetus, causing intellectual disability, microcephaly, and congenital heart defects, even if the fetus has a normal genotype (is a carrier or unaffected). This is not a genetic disease in the fetus; it is a teratogenic effect of the mother's metabolic disorder. The MCAT tests whether you understand that the fetus's genotype does not matter here. The damage comes from the maternal environment.

What You Just Learned From One Disease

Enzyme kinetics (Vmax, cofactors, competitive inhibition at transporters). Amino acid metabolism (Phe to Tyr pathway, transamination producing phenylpyruvate). Essential vs nonessential amino acids (and conditional essentiality). Cofactor biochemistry (BH4, tetrahydrobiopterin). Neurotransmitter synthesis pathways (Tyr to DOPA to dopamine to NE to epi; Trp to serotonin). Blood-brain barrier transport (LAT1 transporter, competitive kinetics). Melanin synthesis. Autosomal recessive inheritance. Newborn screening ethics. Teratogenesis (maternal PKU).

Type 1 Diabetes Mellitus

The body's greatest domino chain, from autoimmunity to every organ system
Polygenic + Autoimmune
Receptor Tyrosine KinasesAutoimmunityMetabolic PathwaysAcid-Base BalanceRenal PhysiologyCardiovascular

Type 1 diabetes is an autoimmune disease in which T cells destroy pancreatic beta cells. It has a strong genetic component (HLA-DR3 and HLA-DR4 associations) but is not strictly Mendelian. The loss of beta cells means loss of insulin. Understanding what insulin does, and what happens without it, connects virtually every metabolic pathway the MCAT tests.

MCAT Concept: Insulin Signaling (Receptor Tyrosine Kinase)

Insulin binds the insulin receptor, a receptor tyrosine kinase (RTK). The receptor autophosphorylates, recruits IRS (insulin receptor substrate), activating two pathways: PI3K/Akt (drives GLUT4 translocation to membrane for glucose uptake, activates glycogen synthase, stimulates lipogenesis, inhibits gluconeogenesis) and Ras/MAPK (promotes cell growth and proliferation). Without insulin, PI3K/Akt is silent: GLUT4 stays intracellular, cells cannot take up glucose, and all downstream metabolic effects are reversed.

Note: brain and liver take up glucose independently of insulin (GLUT1 and GLUT2 are insulin-independent). Red blood cells use GLUT1. Muscle and adipose require insulin-dependent GLUT4. This is why the brain does not starve in diabetes but muscles do.

The Metabolic Catastrophe Without Insulin
No insulin→GLUT4 stays intracellular→Cells starving despite high glucose
Liver "thinks" body is fasting→Ramps up gluconeogenesis→Blood glucose rises even higher
No insulin to inhibit lipolysis→FFAs flood liver→Excess acetyl-CoA (TCA overloaded)→Ketogenesis→DKA
Glucose >180 mg/dL→Exceeds renal threshold→Glycosuria→Osmotic diuresis→Polyuria, polydipsia
MCAT Concept: DKA and Acid-Base

DKA blood gases: low pH (acidosis), low HCO3- (metabolic), low pCO2 (respiratory compensation via Kussmaul breathing). Ketone bodies (acetoacetate, beta-hydroxybutyrate) are organic acids that consume bicarbonate buffer. The anion gap is elevated (Na+ minus Cl+ minus HCO3- is high because unmeasured anions, the ketoacids, are present). This is a high anion gap metabolic acidosis.

Critical trap: serum K+ is often HIGH in DKA (acidosis drives K+ out of cells, insulin deficiency removes the normal stimulus for K+ uptake). But total body K+ is DEPLETED (osmotic diuresis washes it out). When you give insulin, K+ shifts back into cells and the patient can become dangerously hypokalemic. The MCAT loves this: the serum level and total body content of an electrolyte can be in opposite directions.

Long-Term Complications: Every System

Chronic hyperglycemia produces advanced glycation end products (AGEs) that damage the vascular endothelium. Macrovascular: atherosclerosis, MI, stroke. Microvascular: retinopathy (capillary damage in retina), nephropathy (GBM glycosylation, microalbuminuria, eventual ESRD), neuropathy (vasa nervorum damage, peripheral and autonomic). The kidney damage leads to loss of 1-alpha-hydroxylase (no active vitamin D), leading to hypocalcemia, secondary hyperparathyroidism, and osteoporosis. Loss of EPO production causes anemia. Loss of acid excretion causes metabolic acidosis.

What You Just Learned From One Disease

Receptor tyrosine kinase signaling (insulin receptor, PI3K/Akt, Ras/MAPK). GLUT transporter family (insulin-dependent vs independent). Glycolysis, gluconeogenesis, glycogenolysis, lipogenesis, lipolysis, beta-oxidation, ketogenesis (all regulated by insulin). TCA cycle capacity and acetyl-CoA overflow. Acid-base physiology (metabolic acidosis, respiratory compensation, anion gap). Henderson-Hasselbalch in action. Renal threshold and osmotic diuresis. Electrolyte shifts (K+ in acidosis). Autoimmunity and HLA associations. AGE formation and endothelial damage. Renal-endocrine-skeletal connections (vitamin D, PTH, calcium). EPO and anemia. Autonomic neuropathy and baroreceptor reflex.

G6PD Deficiency

The pentose phosphate pathway, made unforgettable through hemolytic crises
X-Linked Recessive
Pentose Phosphate PathwayOxidative StressX-Linked InheritanceRBC MetabolismPharmacogenomicsMalaria Resistance

G6PD (glucose-6-phosphate dehydrogenase) is the rate-limiting enzyme of the pentose phosphate pathway (PPP), also called the hexose monophosphate shunt. This pathway has two critical products: NADPH (used for reductive biosynthesis and to combat oxidative stress) and ribose-5-phosphate (used for nucleotide synthesis). G6PD deficiency is the most common enzymatic disorder in humans, affecting over 400 million people worldwide.

MCAT Concept: Why RBCs Are Uniquely Vulnerable

Red blood cells have no mitochondria, no nucleus, and no ability to synthesize new proteins. They rely entirely on glycolysis for ATP and entirely on the PPP for NADPH. NADPH is essential for maintaining glutathione in its reduced form (GSH). Glutathione reductase uses NADPH to convert oxidized glutathione (GSSG) back to reduced glutathione (GSH). GSH then detoxifies reactive oxygen species (ROS) via glutathione peroxidase. Without G6PD, RBCs cannot make NADPH, cannot regenerate GSH, and are defenseless against oxidative stress. When exposed to oxidants (certain drugs, fava beans, infections), the hemoglobin is oxidized to methemoglobin and then denatures, forming Heinz bodies (precipitates of denatured hemoglobin visible on a blood smear with crystal violet stain). The damaged RBCs are removed by splenic macrophages, which bite out the Heinz bodies, leaving bite cells.

The Chain: No NADPH, No Defense
G6PD deficient→No NADPH from PPP→Cannot reduce GSSG to GSH→ROS accumulate→Hb oxidized→Heinz bodies→Bite cells→Hemolytic anemia

Triggers: primaquine (antimalarial), sulfonamides, dapsone, nitrofurantoin, fava beans (favism), infections (the oxidative burst of neutrophils fighting the infection creates collateral ROS damage).

MCAT Concept: The PPP in Context

The PPP branches from glycolysis at glucose-6-phosphate. The oxidative phase (irreversible) produces 2 NADPH per glucose-6-phosphate and converts it to ribulose-5-phosphate. The non-oxidative phase (reversible) interconverts sugars using transketolase (requires thiamine/B1 as TPP cofactor) and transaldolase, connecting back to glycolytic intermediates (fructose-6-phosphate, glyceraldehyde-3-phosphate).

Rapidly dividing cells (bone marrow, tumors, immune cells) need ribose-5-phosphate for nucleotide synthesis. Cells handling oxidative stress (RBCs, liver, adrenal cortex, lens of eye) need NADPH. The PPP provides both. G6PD deficiency makes the PPP unforgettable because it shows you what happens when this pathway fails.

MCAT Concept: X-Linked Inheritance and Malaria (Again)

G6PD deficiency is X-linked recessive: males are hemizygous and fully affected, females are usually carriers. Like sickle cell trait, G6PD deficiency confers partial resistance to P. falciparum malaria (the parasite is sensitive to oxidative stress, which is elevated in G6PD-deficient RBCs). This explains the high prevalence in malaria-endemic regions: Africa, Mediterranean, Southeast Asia. The MCAT can ask you to compare sickle cell and G6PD as two different genetic strategies for malaria resistance with different inheritance patterns (autosomal recessive vs X-linked recessive).

What You Just Learned From One Disease

Pentose phosphate pathway (oxidative and non-oxidative phases). NADPH production and its critical roles. Glutathione biochemistry (GSH/GSSG cycle, glutathione reductase, glutathione peroxidase). Oxidative stress and reactive oxygen species. RBC metabolism (no mitochondria, glycolysis-dependent, PPP-dependent). Heinz bodies and bite cells on blood smear. X-linked recessive inheritance. Pharmacogenomics (drug-induced hemolysis). Heterozygote advantage and malaria resistance (compare to sickle cell). Thiamine as TPP cofactor in transketolase.

Marfan Syndrome

When the scaffold fails: connective tissue, signaling, and why structure is function
Autosomal Dominant
Extracellular MatrixTGF-beta SignalingAD InheritanceCardiovascular AnatomyProtein StructurePleiotropy

Marfan syndrome is caused by mutations in FBN1, the gene encoding fibrillin-1, a glycoprotein that forms microfibrils in the extracellular matrix. Fibrillin-1 serves two roles: it provides structural support to connective tissues (especially in the aortic wall, suspensory ligament of the lens, and periosteum of long bones), and it sequesters TGF-beta (transforming growth factor beta), keeping it inactive. When fibrillin-1 is defective, TGF-beta is released and becomes overactive, causing tissue destruction.

MCAT Concept: The Extracellular Matrix Is Not Just Scaffolding

The ECM is not passive. It stores growth factors (TGF-beta, FGF, VEGF) and releases them in controlled amounts. Fibrillin-1 microfibrils physically bind TGF-beta and keep it sequestered. When fibrillin is defective, TGF-beta floods the tissue, activating its signaling pathway (TGF-beta binds serine/threonine kinase receptors, which phosphorylate SMAD proteins that translocate to the nucleus and regulate gene expression). Excess TGF-beta in the aortic wall causes smooth muscle apoptosis and elastic fiber degradation, weakening the wall. This is why the most dangerous complication of Marfan is aortic root dilation and dissection.

MCAT Concept: Pleiotropy

Marfan is a perfect example of pleiotropy: one gene affects multiple, seemingly unrelated phenotypes. FBN1 mutation produces: tall stature with long limbs and fingers (arachnodactyly) because periosteal growth is abnormal, lens subluxation (ectopia lentis, typically upward) because the suspensory ligament (zonules) is weakened, aortic root dilation because the aortic wall is weakened, mitral valve prolapse because the valve leaflets are redundant, pectus excavatum or carinatum (chest wall deformities), joint hypermobility, and spontaneous pneumothorax (weakened lung connective tissue). All of these come from one gene defect because fibrillin-1 is everywhere connective tissue exists.

The MCAT may present a patient with several of these features and ask you to identify the underlying genetic defect, or ask which additional finding you would expect.

What You Just Learned From One Disease

Extracellular matrix composition and function (fibrillin, collagen, elastin). ECM as a growth factor reservoir. TGF-beta signaling pathway (serine/threonine kinase receptors, SMADs). Pleiotropy (one gene, many phenotypes). Autosomal dominant inheritance with variable expressivity. Aortic anatomy and the mechanics of dissection. Lens anatomy and suspensory ligaments. Comparison to Ehlers-Danlos (collagen disorders) and homocystinuria (lens down vs up). The concept that structural proteins require dominant mutations to cause disease (dominant negative effect).

Lysosomal Storage Diseases

Tay-Sachs, Gaucher, and Fabry: the cell's recycling center fails, and waste accumulates

Lysosomes contain over 60 hydrolytic enzymes (proteases, lipases, nucleases, glycosidases) that break down cellular waste at an acidic pH (~4.5-5, maintained by H+-ATPase proton pumps). When a single lysosomal enzyme is deficient, its specific substrate accumulates inside the cell, eventually destroying it. The substrate that accumulates tells you which enzyme is missing, and the cell type most affected tells you the clinical presentation.

MCAT Concept: Lysosomal Enzyme Targeting (Mannose-6-Phosphate)

Lysosomal enzymes are synthesized in the rough ER and processed in the Golgi, where they receive a mannose-6-phosphate (M6P) tag. M6P receptors in the trans-Golgi network recognize this tag and direct the enzymes into vesicles destined for lysosomes. In I-cell disease (inclusion cell disease), the enzyme that adds the M6P tag (N-acetylglucosamine-1-phosphotransferase) is defective. ALL lysosomal enzymes are secreted outside the cell instead of being directed to lysosomes. The lysosomes fill with undigested material (inclusions). This disease teaches the entire lysosomal targeting pathway in one step.

MCAT Trap: Cherry-Red Spot

Both Tay-Sachs and Niemann-Pick produce a cherry-red spot on the macula. The red spot is the normal macula showing through because the surrounding retinal ganglion cells are swollen with accumulated lipid (appearing pale). The MCAT may ask you to distinguish them: Tay-Sachs has NO hepatosplenomegaly (GM2 ganglioside accumulates primarily in neurons). Niemann-Pick HAS hepatosplenomegaly (sphingomyelin accumulates in macrophages of liver and spleen too). If the question mentions organ enlargement, think Niemann-Pick. If it is pure neurodegeneration without organ involvement, think Tay-Sachs.

MCAT Concept: Population Genetics in Ashkenazi Jews

Tay-Sachs, Gaucher, and Niemann-Pick are all more common in Ashkenazi Jewish populations (carrier rates as high as 1 in 30 for Tay-Sachs). This is attributed to the founder effect: a small ancestral population carried these alleles at higher-than-average frequency, and the alleles were maintained as the population expanded. Some researchers have proposed heterozygote advantage (similar to sickle cell), though the selective pressure is debated. The MCAT uses these diseases to test population genetics concepts: founder effect, genetic drift, and Hardy-Weinberg calculations in specific populations.

MCAT Concept: Sphingolipid Degradation Pathway

The lysosomal storage diseases teach you the sphingolipid degradation pathway by showing what accumulates when each step is blocked. Sphingolipids are synthesized from ceramide and degraded by sequential removal of sugar residues and head groups. Each enzyme removes one piece, and deficiency of each enzyme causes a different disease. This is the biological equivalent of an assembly line in reverse: if any one worker on the disassembly line is absent, everything before that step piles up.

What You Just Learned From These Diseases

Lysosome function and pH maintenance (H+-ATPase). Enzyme targeting via mannose-6-phosphate (and I-cell disease). Sphingolipid structure and degradation pathway. The relationship between substrate accumulation and clinical phenotype. Cherry-red macula spot mechanism. Hepatosplenomegaly as a sign of macrophage storage. Population genetics: founder effect, genetic drift, Hardy-Weinberg in isolated populations. Autosomal recessive vs X-linked recessive (Fabry is the outlier). Enzyme replacement therapy as treatment principle. Neurodegeneration mechanisms (lipid storage disrupting membrane function).
