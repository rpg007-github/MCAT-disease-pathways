Learn Biology Through Disease

Each disease is a story that naturally teaches multiple foundational concepts

The MCAT does not test whether you memorized a textbook. It tests whether you can think across systems. When a passage describes a patient with kidney disease, the questions might ask about acid-base balance, hormone production, bone density, or blood pressure regulation. The students who struggle are the ones who learned each system in isolation. The students who excel are the ones who understand how everything connects.

The most natural way to learn these connections is through genetic diseases. A disease is what happens when one piece of the biological machine breaks. By understanding the downstream consequences of that break, you learn how all the pieces fit together. A student who truly understands sickle cell disease has, almost without trying, learned protein structure, hemoglobin cooperativity, Mendelian genetics, population genetics, renal physiology, immune function, and cardiovascular biology.

Each disease in this guide was chosen because it teaches the maximum number of MCAT-relevant concepts in a way that is inherently memorable. The guide goes deep enough that you can actually learn from reading it, not just see a list of connections. Simple comprehension checks after each section let you test yourself immediately, and MCAT-style example questions at the end of each disease show you what these concepts look like on test day.

Sickle Cell Disease

A single amino acid substitution that teaches you half of biology
Autosomal Recessive

The Mutation: What Changes and Why It Matters

Everything in sickle cell disease starts with one nucleotide change in the beta-globin gene: GAG becomes GTG. In the genetic code, GAG codes for glutamic acid and GTG codes for valine. These two amino acids could not be more different. Glutamic acid is hydrophilic and negatively charged at physiological pH (its side chain pKa is about 4.1, well below 7.4, so it is deprotonated and carries a negative charge). Valine is hydrophobic and nonpolar (its side chain is a branched hydrocarbon with no charge at any pH).

This matters because the substitution occurs at position 6 of the beta chain, which sits on the surface of the hemoglobin molecule. Normally, the negatively charged glutamic acid at this position faces outward toward the aqueous environment, which is energetically favorable because charged residues interact well with water. When valine replaces it, a hydrophobic "sticky patch" is now exposed on the protein surface. This is the molecular origin of the entire disease.

Why This Teaches Protein Structure

This single disease teaches you all four levels of protein structure simultaneously. The primary structure change (Glu to Val) alters tertiary structure (creates a new hydrophobic patch on the protein surface). Under low-oxygen conditions, this hydrophobic patch on one HbS molecule finds and inserts into a complementary hydrophobic pocket on an adjacent HbS molecule, driving quaternary aggregation into long, rigid fibers. The same forces that normally stabilize protein folding (hydrophobic interactions, van der Waals forces) are now driving pathological polymerization. The rigid HbS polymers push against the red blood cell membrane from inside, deforming it from a flexible biconcave disc into a rigid, crescent-shaped "sickle" cell.

Hemoglobin and Oxygen: The Cooperativity Story

To understand why sickle cells form only under certain conditions, you need to understand how hemoglobin works normally. Hemoglobin is a tetramer made of two alpha subunits and two beta subunits (alpha2-beta2). Each subunit contains a heme group, which is a porphyrin ring with an iron atom (Fe2+) at its center. The iron reversibly binds one molecule of O2, so each hemoglobin tetramer can carry up to four O2 molecules.

Here is the critical concept: hemoglobin exhibits cooperative binding. When the first O2 binds to one subunit, it causes a conformational change in that subunit (from the T state, "tense," low affinity, to the R state, "relaxed," high affinity). Because the four subunits are physically connected, this conformational change is transmitted to the neighboring subunits, making it easier for the next O2 to bind. Each successive O2 binds more easily than the last. This produces the characteristic sigmoidal (S-shaped) oxygen-hemoglobin dissociation curve, as opposed to the hyperbolic curve of myoglobin (which is a single subunit with no cooperativity).

The Hill coefficient quantifies cooperativity. For hemoglobin, the Hill coefficient is about 2.8. A Hill coefficient of 1 means no cooperativity (hyperbolic curve). Greater than 1 means positive cooperativity. The maximum possible Hill coefficient for hemoglobin would be 4 (the number of binding sites), but the actual value is lower because cooperativity is not perfect.

Why This Matters for Sickle Cell

HbS only polymerizes in the deoxygenated (T) state. In the T state, the hydrophobic pocket that accepts the valine sticky patch is exposed. In the R (oxygenated) state, the pocket is hidden inside the protein. This means anything that shifts hemoglobin toward the T state increases sickling:

Low pO2→More T state→HbS polymerizes→Sickling
Low pH (Bohr effect)→H+ stabilizes T state→More sickling
High 2,3-BPG→Stabilizes T state→More sickling
High temperature→Shifts curve right→More O2 released→More T state
High CO2→Stabilizes T via carbamino formation→More sickling

Every factor that shifts the O2-hemoglobin dissociation curve to the right (reduced O2 affinity, more O2 unloading) simultaneously promotes sickling. This is why sickle crises often occur during exercise (low pO2, low pH from lactic acid, high temperature, high CO2), at high altitude (low pO2), or during infections (fever raises temperature).

The Spleen: Why Sickle Cell Patients Get Infections

The spleen is a blood-filtering organ with a unique vascular structure. Blood enters the spleen and passes through narrow sinusoidal slits that are just barely wide enough for a normal, flexible red blood cell to squeeze through. Rigid, sickled RBCs get stuck. In early childhood, sickle cells repeatedly clog and damage the splenic vasculature, causing small infarctions (areas of tissue death from lack of blood supply). By about age 5, the spleen is so scarred that it no longer functions. This is called functional asplenia (the spleen is physically present but non-functional), which eventually progresses to autosplenectomy (the spleen literally shrivels and fibroses).

Why does losing the spleen matter so much? The spleen has a unique immune role: it is the primary site for clearing encapsulated bacteria from the bloodstream. Encapsulated bacteria (like Streptococcus pneumoniae, Haemophilus influenzae, and Neisseria meningitidis) have a polysaccharide capsule that resists phagocytosis. The capsule is slippery; neutrophils and macrophages cannot grab onto it effectively. To kill encapsulated bacteria, the immune system must first coat them with antibodies and complement (a process called opsonization), and the marginal zone B cells of the spleen are the primary producers of the IgM antibodies that target polysaccharide capsule antigens.

Without a functioning spleen, these IgM-producing B cells are lost, opsonization of encapsulated bacteria is impaired, and the patient becomes vulnerable to overwhelming post-splenectomy infection (OPSI). This is a medical emergency that can progress from mild fever to septic shock and death within hours. This is why sickle cell patients receive prophylactic penicillin starting in infancy and pneumococcal vaccines.

The Kidney: Why Sickle Cell Patients Cannot Concentrate Urine

The kidney's ability to concentrate urine depends on the countercurrent multiplier system in the loop of Henle and the countercurrent exchanger in the vasa recta. Here is how it works in a healthy kidney: The descending limb of the loop of Henle is permeable to water but not solutes. As the filtrate descends deeper into the medulla (where the interstitial fluid is increasingly hypertonic), water leaves the descending limb by osmosis, concentrating the filtrate. The ascending limb is impermeable to water but actively pumps out NaCl (via the Na+/K+/2Cl- cotransporter, which is the target of loop diuretics like furosemide). This NaCl accumulates in the medullary interstitium, creating the concentration gradient that drives water out of the descending limb. The result: the medullary interstitium reaches about 1200 mOsm/kg at the tip of the longest loops.

The vasa recta are the blood vessels that supply the renal medulla. They run parallel to the loop of Henle in a U-shaped pattern. As blood descends into the medulla, it loses water and gains solute (equilibrating with the hypertonic medulla). As it ascends, it gains water and loses solute. This countercurrent exchange prevents the blood flow from washing away the carefully built medullary gradient.

Now here is the sickle cell connection: the renal medulla is the perfect environment for sickling. It has low oxygen tension (the medulla receives only about 10% of renal blood flow and is relatively hypoxic), high osmolality (1200 mOsm/kg, which dehydrates RBCs and concentrates HbS), and acidic pH (H+ secretion in the collecting duct acidifies the surrounding interstitium). Sickled RBCs in the vasa recta block blood flow, causing microinfarctions of the medullary tissue. Over time, the countercurrent multiplier is destroyed. Without the medullary gradient, the kidney cannot concentrate urine above plasma osmolality (~300 mOsm/kg). This is called isosthenuria.

The practical consequence: sickle cell patients produce large volumes of dilute urine even when dehydrated. They cannot conserve water. Dehydration concentrates their blood, increases HbS concentration within RBCs, and promotes more sickling, creating a vicious cycle.

Population Genetics: Why the Allele Persists

If sickle cell disease is so damaging, why has the HbS allele not been eliminated by natural selection? The answer is heterozygote advantage (also called balancing selection or overdominance). In regions where Plasmodium falciparum malaria is endemic, individuals with sickle cell trait (HbAS, heterozygous) have a survival advantage over both homozygotes. HbAA individuals are fully susceptible to malaria. HbSS individuals have sickle cell disease. HbAS individuals are partially protected from malaria because the parasite does not thrive as well in cells containing some HbS (sickling of infected RBCs leads to their premature removal by the spleen before the parasite can complete its lifecycle).

This means the heterozygote has the highest fitness in malaria-endemic environments. Natural selection maintains both alleles in the population: the A allele because AA is better than SS, and the S allele because AS is better than AA in the presence of malaria. The allele frequencies reach a stable equilibrium determined by the relative fitness costs of malaria vs. sickle cell disease.

This is directly tied to Hardy-Weinberg. If the frequency of the S allele is q, then: frequency of HbSS (disease) = q², frequency of HbAS (trait, carrier) = 2pq, and frequency of HbAA (normal) = p². If the MCAT tells you that 1 in 400 individuals in a population has sickle cell disease, you can calculate q: q² = 1/400, so q = 1/20 = 0.05, p = 0.95, and the carrier frequency is 2(0.95)(0.05) = 0.095, or about 1 in 10.5.

The Protein: CFTR Is a Chloride Channel

CFTR stands for cystic fibrosis transmembrane conductance regulator. Despite the name suggesting it "regulates" something, CFTR is itself a chloride channel. It sits in the apical (outward-facing) membrane of epithelial cells lining the airways, intestines, pancreatic ducts, sweat glands, and reproductive tract. When open, it allows chloride ions (Cl-) to flow out of the cell and into the lumen.

An important distinction: CFTR is a channel, not a pump. Channels allow ions to flow passively down their electrochemical gradient. Pumps use energy (usually ATP) to move ions against their gradient. However, CFTR is unusual because it requires ATP binding and hydrolysis to gate (open) the channel. Specifically, protein kinase A (PKA) phosphorylates CFTR's regulatory domain, and then ATP binding to the nucleotide-binding domains opens the pore. So ATP is used for gating, not for transport. The actual chloride movement is passive.

Why does chloride secretion matter? Wherever chloride goes, sodium follows (driven by the electrical gradient created by Cl- movement), and water follows sodium (by osmosis). So CFTR-mediated chloride secretion is really the master switch for fluid secretion. When CFTR is absent, chloride stays inside the cell, sodium and water stay with it, and the secretions on the epithelial surface become thick and dehydrated.

The Mutation: Why Delta-F508 Never Reaches the Membrane

The most common CF mutation is delta-F508: a deletion of phenylalanine at position 508. This does not completely destroy CFTR function. If you could somehow force the mutant protein to reach the cell surface, it would actually function as a chloride channel (though with reduced open probability). The problem is that the deletion causes the protein to misfold during synthesis in the endoplasmic reticulum.

Here is what normally happens: CFTR is synthesized by ribosomes on the rough ER. As the polypeptide emerges, it is threaded into the ER membrane (CFTR is a multi-pass transmembrane protein). ER-resident chaperone proteins (like calnexin, calreticulin, and Hsp70) assist folding and check quality. Properly folded CFTR is packaged into transport vesicles and sent to the Golgi apparatus, where it is further glycosylated (sugar modifications). From the trans-Golgi, it is shipped to the plasma membrane via secretory vesicles.

The delta-F508 protein does not fold correctly. The ER quality control system detects this and targets it for ER-associated degradation (ERAD): the misfolded protein is retrotranslocated out of the ER back into the cytoplasm, tagged with ubiquitin (a small protein attached by E1, E2, and E3 ubiquitin ligases), and degraded by the 26S proteasome. The channel never reaches the cell surface.

Why This Teaches the Entire Secretory Pathway

Understanding CF means understanding: rough ER and co-translational insertion via SRP (signal recognition particle), ER chaperones and protein quality control, vesicular transport from ER to Golgi (COPII-coated vesicles), glycosylation in ER (core glycosylation) and Golgi (terminal glycosylation), ERAD and retrotranslocation, the ubiquitin-proteasome degradation pathway (E1/E2/E3 enzymes, K48-linked polyubiquitin as a degradation signal), and the final exocytic pathway from trans-Golgi to plasma membrane. All from one mutation in one protein.

The Organs: One Mechanism, Many Systems

Lungs: Without CFTR, the airway surface liquid (the thin layer of fluid on top of the airway epithelium) becomes dehydrated and viscous. Normally, cilia beat in a coordinated wave to sweep mucus and trapped particles up and out of the lungs (the mucociliary escalator). In CF, the mucus is too thick for the cilia to move effectively. Bacteria (especially Pseudomonas aeruginosa and Staphylococcus aureus) become trapped in the stagnant mucus and establish chronic infections. The immune system responds with massive neutrophil infiltration, and the neutrophil-released enzymes (elastase, proteases) and DNA (from dead neutrophils) actually make the mucus even thicker. Over years, the chronic inflammation damages the airway walls, causing permanent dilation of the bronchi called bronchiectasis. Respiratory failure is the primary cause of death in CF.

Pancreas: The pancreatic ducts are lined with CFTR-expressing epithelial cells that normally secrete chloride and bicarbonate (CFTR also conducts bicarbonate) to create a watery, alkaline fluid that carries digestive enzymes from the acinar cells into the duodenum. Without CFTR, these secretions are thick, and the ducts become plugged. Enzymes (lipase, amylase, trypsin) cannot reach the small intestine. The enzymes back up and can actually digest the pancreas itself (autodigestion), leading to fibrosis and scarring over time. The result is pancreatic insufficiency: patients cannot properly digest food, particularly fats.

Fat malabsorption has cascading consequences. Without pancreatic lipase in the duodenum, dietary triglycerides are not broken down into fatty acids and monoglycerides, so they cannot be absorbed. Fat-soluble vitamins (A, D, E, K) are dissolved in dietary fat, so they are lost too. Each deficiency has specific consequences: Vitamin A deficiency causes night blindness (retinal is derived from vitamin A and is required for rhodopsin in rod cells). Vitamin D deficiency impairs calcium absorption from the gut, leading to rickets in children or osteomalacia in adults. Vitamin E is an antioxidant that protects cell membranes from lipid peroxidation; deficiency causes neurological problems and hemolytic anemia. Vitamin K is required for gamma-carboxylation of glutamic acid residues on clotting factors II, VII, IX, and X; deficiency causes prolonged bleeding.

Sweat glands: This is where the diagnostic test comes from. In normal sweat glands, the secretory coil produces a salty fluid (isotonic with plasma). As the sweat travels up the duct toward the skin surface, CFTR and ENaC (epithelial sodium channel) reabsorb NaCl from the sweat, making it hypotonic (less salty) by the time it reaches the surface. In CF, CFTR does not function in the duct, so NaCl is not reabsorbed, and the sweat remains salty. The sweat chloride test (chloride concentration >60 mEq/L is diagnostic) is the gold standard for CF diagnosis. Notice that in the sweat gland, CFTR's role is absorption, while in the lungs and pancreas its role is secretion. The direction depends on the electrochemical gradient in each tissue.

Reproductive tract: Approximately 98% of males with CF are infertile due to congenital bilateral absence of the vas deferens (CBAVD). The vas deferens is the duct that carries sperm from the epididymis to the ejaculatory duct. In CF, thick secretions in the developing vas deferens cause it to degenerate before birth. Sperm are produced normally in the testes but have no way to reach the ejaculate. Some men with CBAVD as their only symptom are found to carry CF mutations, making this an important clinical connection.

The Cholera Connection: CFTR at the Center of GPCR Signaling

This is one of the most beautiful connections in all of biology for the MCAT. Cholera toxin is produced by Vibrio cholerae bacteria in the small intestine. The toxin enters intestinal epithelial cells and ADP-ribosylates the Gs alpha subunit of a G-protein coupled receptor (GPCR). Normally, Gs alpha activates adenylyl cyclase briefly, then hydrolyzes its GTP to GDP and turns itself off. Cholera toxin prevents this GTP hydrolysis, locking Gs alpha in the permanently active state. Adenylyl cyclase runs continuously, converting ATP to cAMP without stopping. cAMP activates protein kinase A (PKA), which phosphorylates and opens CFTR chloride channels. Massive chloride secretion into the intestinal lumen drags sodium and water out, producing the profuse, watery ("rice water") diarrhea that can kill through dehydration in hours.

This is the exact opposite of CF: cholera is too much CFTR activity, CF is no CFTR activity. There is even a hypothesis that CF carriers (heterozygous, one functional CFTR copy) have reduced CFTR activity that makes them partially resistant to cholera-induced fluid loss, providing a heterozygote advantage similar to sickle cell trait and malaria.

The Mutation: CAG Repeats and Toxic Protein

The huntingtin gene on chromosome 4 contains a stretch of CAG repeats. In normal individuals, this stretch is 10-35 repeats long. In Huntington disease, it expands to 36 or more (full penetrance at 40+). Since CAG codes for glutamine, the mutant huntingtin protein contains a long polyglutamine (polyQ) tract. This polyQ stretch causes the protein to misfold and form intranuclear aggregates (clumps of misfolded protein inside the nucleus) that are toxic to neurons. The mechanism of toxicity involves disrupting normal transcription, interfering with protein quality control systems (the proteasome becomes overwhelmed), and triggering apoptosis.

The neurons most vulnerable are the GABAergic medium spiny neurons of the caudate nucleus and putamen (together called the striatum). Why these neurons are selectively vulnerable despite huntingtin being expressed in all cells remains an active area of research, but it likely relates to their high metabolic demands and sensitivity to excitotoxicity (excessive glutamate stimulation).

Anticipation: Getting Worse Each Generation

Trinucleotide repeats are unstable during DNA replication. During replication of the repeat region, the two DNA strands can slip relative to each other (a process called replication slippage). If the newly synthesized strand slips forward and re-anneals out of register, the new strand will have extra repeats. This is more likely during spermatogenesis than oogenesis because spermatogenesis involves many more rounds of mitotic cell division (spermatogonia divide continuously throughout a man's life, while oogenesis involves far fewer divisions).

The consequence: a father with 42 repeats may pass on 50+ repeats to his child. More repeats means more toxic polyQ protein, which means earlier onset and more severe disease. This generational worsening is called anticipation. A grandmother might develop symptoms at age 55, her son at age 40, and her grandson at age 25. If the MCAT shows a pedigree where each generation is diagnosed younger than the last, the answer is anticipation from trinucleotide repeat expansion.

The Basal Ganglia: Why Patients Cannot Stop Moving

The basal ganglia are a group of subcortical nuclei that regulate voluntary movement. They do not initiate movement (that is the motor cortex). Instead, they act as a filter that selects desired movements and suppresses unwanted ones. Think of the basal ganglia as a sophisticated gate: they decide which motor signals from the cortex are allowed to proceed and which are blocked.

There are two pathways through the basal ganglia. The direct pathway facilitates movement: cortex excites the striatum, the striatum inhibits the globus pallidus interna (GPi), and since GPi normally inhibits the thalamus, inhibiting GPi releases (disinhibits) the thalamus, which then excites the motor cortex to produce movement. Think of it as removing the brake. The indirect pathway suppresses movement: the striatum inhibits the globus pallidus externa (GPe), which normally inhibits the subthalamic nucleus (STN). With GPe inhibited, STN is active, and it excites GPi, which inhibits the thalamus. The net effect is less thalamic excitation of the cortex, meaning movement is suppressed. Think of it as applying the brake.

In Huntington disease, the GABAergic neurons of the indirect pathway are the first to degenerate. Without the indirect pathway applying the brake, the thalamus is excessively active, sending too many excitatory signals to the motor cortex. The result is chorea: involuntary, random, dance-like movements that the patient cannot control. The patient's body is constantly moving because the brake system is broken.

Compare this to Parkinson disease: in Parkinson, dopaminergic neurons in the substantia nigra pars compacta die. These neurons normally excite the direct pathway (via D1 receptors) and inhibit the indirect pathway (via D2 receptors). Losing them means the direct pathway is underactive (less "go" signal) and the indirect pathway is overactive (more "stop" signal). The result is the opposite of Huntington: difficulty initiating movement (bradykinesia), rigidity, resting tremor, and shuffling gait.

Autosomal Recessive

The Blocked Pathway

Phenylalanine hydroxylase (PAH) converts phenylalanine to tyrosine. It requires the cofactor tetrahydrobiopterin (BH4) and molecular oxygen. Without PAH, phenylalanine accumulates to toxic levels while tyrosine becomes deficient. The excess phenylalanine is shunted into an alternative pathway: transamination converts it to phenylpyruvate (the "phenylketone" that gives the disease its name), which is excreted in the urine and gives it a musty odor.

Tyrosine is normally a nonessential amino acid because the body makes it from phenylalanine. In PKU, tyrosine becomes conditionally essential because the conversion pathway is blocked. This has consequences for everything tyrosine is used for: melanin synthesis (PKU patients have fair skin and light hair), and neurotransmitter synthesis (tyrosine is the precursor to DOPA, then dopamine, then norepinephrine, then epinephrine).

The brain damage in PKU comes from two mechanisms. First, phenylalanine is directly toxic to developing neurons. Second, excess phenylalanine in the blood competes with other large neutral amino acids (tyrosine, tryptophan, leucine, isoleucine, valine) for the LAT1 transporter across the blood-brain barrier. This is competitive inhibition in a physiological context: the transporter has a finite number of binding sites, and when Phe saturates them, other amino acids cannot get through. Reduced tryptophan in the brain means reduced serotonin synthesis. Reduced tyrosine means reduced catecholamine synthesis.

Type 1 Diabetes

The body's greatest domino chain: autoimmunity to every organ system
Polygenic + Autoimmune

Insulin Signaling: The Master Metabolic Switch

Insulin is released by pancreatic beta cells when blood glucose rises (after a meal). It binds the insulin receptor, a receptor tyrosine kinase (RTK). Upon insulin binding, the receptor autophosphorylates tyrosine residues on its intracellular domain. These phosphotyrosines serve as docking sites for IRS (insulin receptor substrate) proteins, which activate two major pathways.

The PI3K/Akt pathway handles metabolism: it causes GLUT4 glucose transporters to translocate from intracellular vesicles to the cell membrane (allowing glucose uptake into muscle and fat), activates glycogen synthase (storing glucose as glycogen), stimulates lipogenic enzymes (making fat from excess glucose), and inhibits gluconeogenic enzymes in the liver (stopping glucose production). The Ras/MAPK pathway handles growth: it promotes cell proliferation and differentiation.

Critical point: not all tissues need insulin for glucose uptake. The brain uses GLUT1 (insulin-independent, always present on the cell surface). The liver uses GLUT2 (insulin-independent, always present, bidirectional). Red blood cells use GLUT1. Muscle and adipose tissue use GLUT4 (insulin-dependent). This is why in diabetes, the brain does not starve (it gets glucose regardless of insulin), but muscles and fat tissue are glucose-deprived despite high blood glucose.

DKA: The Metabolic Emergency

Without insulin, the liver behaves as though the body is starving (because insulin is the "fed state" signal). It ramps up gluconeogenesis (making glucose from amino acids and glycerol), raising blood glucose even higher. Insulin normally inhibits lipolysis in adipose tissue; without it, free fatty acids (FFAs) flood the bloodstream. The liver converts FFAs to acetyl-CoA via beta-oxidation, but the TCA cycle cannot process all of it (the TCA cycle intermediates are being drained off for gluconeogenesis). The excess acetyl-CoA is shunted to ketogenesis, producing acetoacetate, beta-hydroxybutyrate, and acetone. These ketone bodies are organic acids that consume bicarbonate buffer and lower blood pH. This is diabetic ketoacidosis (DKA).

G6PD Deficiency

The pentose phosphate pathway made unforgettable
X-Linked Recessive

Why Red Blood Cells Are Uniquely Vulnerable

Red blood cells have no mitochondria (they were ejected during maturation to make room for more hemoglobin), no nucleus, and no ability to make new proteins. They rely on glycolysis for all their ATP and on the pentose phosphate pathway (PPP) for all their NADPH. G6PD is the first and rate-limiting enzyme of the PPP's oxidative phase. It converts glucose-6-phosphate to 6-phosphogluconate while reducing NADP+ to NADPH.

NADPH has one critical job in RBCs: it keeps glutathione in its reduced form (GSH). Here is the chain: NADPH is used by glutathione reductase to convert oxidized glutathione (GSSG) back to reduced glutathione (GSH). GSH is then used by glutathione peroxidase to neutralize reactive oxygen species (ROS) like hydrogen peroxide (H2O2), converting them to water. Without G6PD, there is no NADPH. Without NADPH, glutathione cannot be regenerated. Without glutathione, ROS accumulate and oxidize hemoglobin, first to methemoglobin (Fe3+ instead of Fe2+, cannot bind O2), then to denatured aggregates called Heinz bodies. Heinz bodies stick to the RBC membrane, making the cell rigid. The spleen recognizes these rigid cells and removes them, or splenic macrophages "bite out" the Heinz body inclusions, leaving bite cells.

Marfan Syndrome

Connective tissue, TGF-beta, and pleiotropy
Autosomal Dominant

Marfan is caused by mutations in FBN1 (fibrillin-1), a glycoprotein that forms microfibrils in the extracellular matrix. Fibrillin-1 does two things: provides structural support and sequesters TGF-beta. When fibrillin is defective, TGF-beta is released and overactivates its signaling pathway (serine/threonine kinase receptors that phosphorylate SMAD transcription factors). Excess TGF-beta causes smooth muscle apoptosis in the aortic wall, elastic fiber degradation, and tissue remodeling. The most dangerous complication is aortic root dilation and dissection.

Marfan is a textbook example of pleiotropy: one gene produces multiple, seemingly unrelated phenotypes. Because fibrillin-1 is everywhere connective tissue exists, the FBN1 mutation affects the skeletal system (tall stature, long limbs, arachnodactyly, pectus deformity, joint hypermobility), the eyes (lens subluxation upward because the zonular ligaments that suspend the lens are weakened), the cardiovascular system (aortic root dilation, mitral valve prolapse), and the lungs (spontaneous pneumothorax from weakened visceral pleura).

Lysosomal Storage Diseases

Tay-Sachs, Gaucher, and the cell's recycling center
Autosomal Recessive (most)

Lysosomes are membrane-bound organelles containing 60+ hydrolytic enzymes that operate at pH ~5 (maintained by V-type H+-ATPase proton pumps that actively transport H+ into the lysosome). Each enzyme degrades a specific substrate. When one enzyme is missing, its substrate accumulates inside the lysosome, swelling the cell and eventually destroying it. The clinical presentation depends on which cell type is most affected.

Lysosomal enzymes reach the lysosome via the mannose-6-phosphate (M6P) pathway. In the cis-Golgi, the enzyme N-acetylglucosamine-1-phosphotransferase adds a phosphate to mannose residues on lysosomal enzyme surfaces. M6P receptors in the trans-Golgi recognize this tag and direct the enzymes into clathrin-coated vesicles bound for lysosomes. In I-cell disease (inclusion cell disease), the phosphotransferase is defective, so ALL lysosomal enzymes lack the M6P tag, are not directed to lysosomes, and are secreted outside the cell. The lysosomes fill with undigested material. High levels of lysosomal enzymes are found in the patient's blood (they were secreted instead of retained).
